Pursuing the Heart

Sacha – The Self-Fulfilling Prophesy of the Human Doughnut

Many of us are living with a broken paradigm: sincerely feeling & believing that we have a gaping hole in our soul. A vague sensation that we’re incomplete. But wait! If we can get that dream job, we’ll finally be happy. Or maybe once we meet that special someone, fall in love & la di da. You get the idea. While that certainly makes a great template for movies — which is probably where we got this notion from — it’s a misguided way to live.

Ever heard of the Hedonic treadmill? That’s what makes lottery winners miserable again after a few months. You can literally win a lifetime of material rewards, and still be a human doughnut. Ouch.

Lesson learned. No amount of “stuff” will plug this hole. The more we get, the more we want. The more we want, the less we’re happy. Repeat.

I suggest this: don’t be mad at the hole, it’s just doing it’s job. Holes are made for good stuff to disappear into, much like black holes absorb light or your mouth absorbs avocado. Nevertheless, the hole in our soul is a problem. I agree. So what are we going to do about it?

Reversing The Flow

In this scenario, happiness flows in from the outside, and disappears into the hole. The solution is to reverse the flow. If the old paradigm is to perpetually seek, take, hoard & protect until the hole is filled (energy flowing in), the new paradigm is to give, provide, share and love (energy flowing out). This turns a hole into a source.

This is hard. When we’re in a place of lack (doughnut mode), our instinct is to put ourselves first, at all costs. Survival mode. It seems absurd to think that helping others will help us. It seems counter-intuitive — even paradoxical — that giving makes us richer.

Think of it this way, if it were obvious, everyone would be doing it. Countless advanced teachings are veiled in the paradoxical, which throws us off their scent. The mistake we make is assuming that the emotional/spiritual world functions with the same rules as the material world. Though they are interconnected (we’ll talk about this in another edition), their principles are sometimes diametrically opposed.

Here’s the underlying dynamic:

By giving of ourselves, of our caring, of our help, of our attention, we are implying that we have more than enough to share. This is powerful. Implicitly, our mental model of ourselves changes from a doughnut to that of an overflowing source of love. From lack of abundance, inner poverty & being a taker, to inner riches & becoming a genuine giver. A good trade, if you ask me.
